Upcycled Polyhedron Lamp

Recycled chipboard and boba straws, 2015

This lamp was made entirely from other people's trash, which I scavenged from curbsides and dumpsters. The project was inspired by the socio-economic inequality in the design world as well as the open design movement.

The frame of the lamp is laser-cut recycled chipboard, and the panes are used boba straws. The socket was taken from an old lamp and the cord and plug were cut from an old television.

The lamp can rest on any of its faces, for variations in form and light tint and intensity.

The replicable design can be found on Instructables.com here.

Archaeological Illustration

Rapidograph on Mylar, 2018

These are a collection of technical drawings of recently recovered Etruscan artifacts from the Poggio Civitate Archeological Field School in Vescovado, Italy, where I interned as an archaeological illustrator during summer 2017.

My work consisted mainly of producing detailed technical stipple drawings of Etruscan artifacts recovered at the site, and I also aided in the production of reconstruction images and excavated in the trenches.

Previous Next

The artifacts I documented consisted of many spindle whorls and spindle whorl fragments, used in the production of thread, as well as many plaster fragments from buildings, and some pottery sherds. The original drawings are between 3" x 3" and 6" x 8" in size, drawn at the scale of the artifacts themselves (scales and labels not shown).

I was on a team of five illustrators, and we worked closely with the conservator and the head of the dig to ensure that we produced drawings demonstrating an understanding of the artifacts and their original purposes and highlighting features that are of importance to archaeologists.

Bending

Bending is a book created by myself and fellow Brown student Shannon Kingsley as part of our 2018 summer research fellowship under the National Science Foundation.

We were tasked with creating a visual platform to inform the Rhode Island public about the impacts of climate change, specifically upon salt marsh habitats. We worked in conjunction with the Brown University Herbarium and the Edna Lawrence Nature Lab, and we compiled visuals and research through visiting the field, cross-referencing herbarium records, and using cutting-edge imaging technology both at local salt marshes and in the lab using collected samples.

The book is written in a narrative manner, following our discoveries throughout the summer to ultimately educate readers on herbaria, salt marsh ecosystems, and natural history. We produced all the visuals in the book, including DSLR photographs, drone photography, macropod images, cyanotypes, diagrams, and illustrations. Shannon focused on writing while I took the lead on design.

The following are sample spreads from the book, which will be printed and disseminated to Rhode Island schools, libraries, and universities this spring.

"It's a Joke"

Silkscreen Series, 2018

This is a series of three silkscreened prints.

The subject of each composition is a close friend who lives with depression. The quote on the bottom of each print is something that each friend told me, and the figures are placed in the spaces in which they said them. This series points out the humor that sometimes masks mental health, to make prints which, like some people's experienced realities, might be disturbing or funny or both.

Feast

Oil on Canvas, 82" x 47", 2018

This is an oil painting I made during the fall semester of my fourth year, as an independent study project with professor Eric Telfort. The original goal of the project was to brush up on my painting skills and improve technically, but the painting evolved into a very personal and monumental piece.

The sheep at the center of this composition is surrounded by elements holding personal and symbolic meaning about memory and identity. At its core, this piece is about honoring family tradition and exploring notions of beauty.

Empathy

Oil on Illustration Board, 32" x 40", 2018

This is an oil painting I made after a visit to the pediatric operating room to watch a procedure on a young boy for a medical illustration course. In the moment, I was struck by the sight of the child laid out on the table, and by my emotional response to his youth, fragility, and vulnerability. I created this painting to convey the idea of the human ideal not necessarily being health versus sickness, but being empathy.

About the Artist

Nadia is a senior in the Brown|RISD Dual Degree Program, studying Illustration and Computer Science, with a focus in visual computing. She pursues fine arts as well as illustration, and is exploring how her artistic practice and her studies in computer graphics inform one another. In her work, she explores themes of contrast as applied to her identity as an American Muslim woman and her social environment, the discrepancy between appearances and internal reality, and the dichotomy of the gritty and mundane with the beautiful and monumental. Her attention to detail is evident in her fine art pieces and is crucial for her technical work, including professional experience in scientific illustration.
